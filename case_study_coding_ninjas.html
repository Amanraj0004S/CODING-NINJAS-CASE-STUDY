01 · Executive Summary

The Business Problem

A significant percentage of highly engaged, non-technical professionals — HR Managers, Sales Leaders, Logistics Heads with 5–12 years of experience — drop off at the landing page and checkout stage after completing the initial webinar for a ₹1.5 Lakh Data Analytics Job Bootcamp.

These are high-LTV adult learners with genuine purchasing power. They have already invested 45 minutes in a webinar. Their intent is real. The product is right for them. But the funnel fails them — and the business loses a disproportionately valuable segment.

The Root Problem in One Line

The current funnel is a "One-Size-Fits-All" experience heavily skewed towards 21-year-old CS students. Senior non-tech users suffer from a Contextual Trust Deficit and Imposter Syndrome — fearing the curriculum is too technical and irrelevant to their specific domain.

North Star Metric: Checkout Intent Rate for Non-Technical Professionals — the percentage of non-tech users who arrive on the post-webinar landing page and complete the ₹5,000 booking reservation.

02 · Context & The Friction Point

Where the Funnel Breaks

The product: a ₹1.5 Lakh premium Data Analytics Job Bootcamp. The friction point: the post-webinar landing page and lead capture form.

Current State — What the Page Shows
  • —Generic technical jargon: Python, SQL, Jupyter, Pandas
  • —Placement hikes for entry-level tech roles at IT service companies
  • —Stories of 22-year-old fresh graduates doubling their first salary
  • —Single generic lead form: Name, Email, Phone — identical for all user types
What the Non-Tech Senior User Sees

A page that says: "This is not for you. This is for someone 10 years younger with a different background. If you enroll, you will start at the bottom." That is the unintended message. It is never written. It is always felt.

The impact: loss of high-LTV adult learners who have the purchasing power, the professional urgency, and the intent — but not the domain-specific confidence to proceed.

03 · Root Cause Analysis — The 5 Whys

Why Does She Drop Off?

Problem Statement

A 32-year-old HR Manager clicks a high-intent ad, watches a 45-minute webinar, but bounces off the landing page without booking a consultation or paying the ₹5,000 reservation fee.

Why #1
Why does she drop off?
Because she feels intense Imposter Syndrome and fears the curriculum is designed for junior software engineers — not for someone with her background.
Why #2
Why does she feel it's for junior engineers?
Because the landing page predominantly features generic "Data Analyst" placement stories at IT service companies, highlighting raw technical skills rather than business outcomes or domain-specific applications.
Why #3
Why does highlighting technical skills scare her?
Because as a non-technical professional, she assumes "Data Analytics" means erasing her 10 years of HR experience and starting over at the bottom of the corporate ladder — a prospect that is both financially and psychologically threatening.
Why #4
Why does she assume she has to start over?
Because the product marketing funnel entirely fails to explain the concept of "Domain Upskilling" (e.g., HR Analytics, Sales Ops Analytics) versus "Career Switching" (Generic Data Analyst). These are fundamentally different value propositions — and they are treated identically.
Root Cause — Why #5
Why does the funnel fail to explain this?
The current user journey is a static, One-Size-Fits-All funnel. Regardless of background, intent, or domain, all users are pushed through the exact same landing page. This is a total Contextual Relevance Failure — and it costs the business its most valuable segment.
04 · User Psychology — Jobs-To-Be-Done

What Is She Actually Hiring This Bootcamp To Do?

The JTBD framework forces us to look beyond stated preferences to the underlying progress the user is trying to make. The gap between stated and latent need is where the conversion problem lives.

Stated Need

"I need to learn Data Analytics."

Latent Need — The Actual Job-To-Be-Done

"When I am overwhelmed by manual operations and stagnant salary growth in my mid-career, I want to acquire a high-leverage technical skill that integrates with my existing domain knowledge — so I can transition into a strategic, higher-paying, desk-based leadership role without starting over as a junior."

The implication: the product is not being marketed as a leadership accelerator for domain experts. It is being marketed as a technical reskilling program for career changers. These require entirely different messaging, social proof, and conversion mechanics.

05 · Proposed Solution — The Dynamic Persona Funnel

The Interactive SPA Architecture

To solve the Contextual Trust Deficit, the post-webinar user journey must transition from a static brochure to an interactive, persona-driven diagnosis. The solution is a Single Page Application (SPA) flow with five sequential steps:

01
Filter First — The Hook
Remove all technical jargon from the hero section. Replace with a single, low-friction binary choice: [ College Student ] vs [ Working Professional ]. This one question segments 80% of intent variance.
02
The Intent Fork
If Working Professional is selected, present a strategic fork: [ Supercharge Current Career ] vs [ Complete Career Switch ]. Add a UX nudge: "Pro-Tip — domain upskilling typically yields a faster, higher salary jump than a full career switch." This anchors the user's decision and reduces the Imposter Syndrome threshold.
03
The Personalisation Engine — Core Feature
If Supercharge is selected, the user chooses their domain from a clean 2×4 interactive grid: HR · Finance · Marketing · Operations · Tech · Consulting · Sales · Logistics. This is the moment the product becomes personal. Each domain selection loads a different content universe.
04
Dynamic Lead Capture — The Kill Shot
The final lead capture form updates its headline, social proof, and CTA copy based on the selected domain. Example (HR selected): "Take the first step towards becoming a Strategic HR Analytics Lead. Average Market CTC: ₹15LPA+" — with HR-specific placement stories below. The user is no longer looking at a generic form. They are looking at a mirror.
05
The Content Stitch — Delivery
Upon registration, the backend stitches a pre-recorded, 10-minute domain-specific intro video to the front of the standard live bootcamp presentation. An HR registrant receives: "How Data Analytics is Transforming HR" before the general curriculum begins. The product feels custom before a single class is attended.
